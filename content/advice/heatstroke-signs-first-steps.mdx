Heatstroke is one of the few true emergencies a healthy pet can fall into on an ordinary day, and in the UAE the window for it is long — from May well into October. The good news: it announces itself, and the early signs are easy to learn. Knowing them, and knowing the first steps, can save a life.

One thing before anything else: **heatstroke is always a vet emergency.** The steps below are what you do on the way — they are never a substitute for getting there.

## Who is most at risk

Any cat or dog can overheat, but some need extra watching:

- **Flat-faced breeds** — bulldogs, pugs, Persians and similar. Their short airways make cooling by panting much harder.
- **Overweight, elderly, or very young animals.**
- **Thick-coated dogs** bred for cold places.
- **Any dog exercising outdoors** in the warm months, even briefly. Most heatstroke cases here start as a normal walk.

## Know the danger signs

**In dogs:**

- Heavy, frantic panting that doesn't settle with rest
- Thick, ropey drool
- Gums that turn bright red, or later pale or bluish
- Weakness, stumbling, or collapsing
- Vomiting or diarrhoea
- Confusion — not responding to their name, staring through you

**In cats:**

- **Panting at all.** Cats pant so rarely that an open-mouthed, breathing-hard cat should be treated as an emergency until a vet says otherwise.
- Drooling or a wet chin
- Restless pacing that becomes lethargy
- Stumbling or lying flat and unresponsive

## What to do first

1. **Move them out of the heat immediately** — into shade at minimum, into air conditioning if you can.
2. **Offer cool water to drink, but never force it.** Small amounts are fine; don't pour water into the mouth of a weak or dazed animal.
3. **Cool the body gradually.** Wet their paws, belly and ears with cool — not iced — water. A damp towel laid under them helps. Avoid ice baths and freezing water: cooling too fast causes its own problems.
4. **Get moving to the vet while you do all of this.** Call ahead if you can so they're ready for you. Even a pet who seems to recover can have internal effects that only a vet can find, so the trip matters even when the panting stops.

## Preventing it in the first place

- Walk dogs before sunrise or well after sunset in summer — and keep summer walks shorter than winter ones.
- Never leave a pet in a parked car, even for minutes, even in shade, even with a window open. Cars here become ovens astonishingly fast.
- Make sure outdoor water bowls are deep, ceramic and in all-day shade.
- On the hottest days, the kindest walk is a short one — or a game indoors instead.

Heat is part of life here, and pets live long, happy lives in the UAE — it just asks a little more awareness of the people who love them.
