On a 40°C afternoon, asphalt in direct sun can reach over 60°C — hot enough to burn skin, and paw pads are skin. Burnt pads are painful, slow to heal, and entirely preventable. All it takes is one habit before every summer walk.

## The 7-second test

**Press the back of your hand flat against the pavement and hold it there for seven seconds.** If it's too hot for your hand, it's too hot for their paws. That's the whole test — no thermometer, no guesswork. The back of the hand is more sensitive than the palm, which is exactly why we use it.

Test the actual surface you'll walk on: asphalt, paving stones and sand all hold heat differently, and a road that passed at 7 am can fail by 9.

## When to walk in summer

- **Before sunrise is the gold standard.** The ground has had all night to cool.
- **Well after sunset is second best** — but remember the pavement stays hot for hours after the sun goes down. An 7 pm pavement can still fail the test in July. Test it; don't assume.
- **Grass and shaded sand are kinder surfaces** when you can find them.
- On the very hottest days, a shorter walk plus play indoors is a perfectly good day for a dog.

## Balcony tiles count too

This one is easy to miss: **tiles on a sunny balcony or terrace get just as hot as pavement.** If your cat or dog spends time out there, do the same 7-second test on the tiles — and make sure there's always a shaded, cooler patch and water available. Better yet, keep balcony time to mornings and evenings in summer.

## Know the danger signs

A dog won't always show you a burn right away. After a summer walk, watch for:

- **Limping, or refusing to walk** — especially suddenly, mid-walk
- **Licking or chewing at the paws**
- Pads that look darker than usual, red, or blistered
- Pieces of pad that look loose or torn

If you see blistering, open skin, or a dog in clear pain: rinse the paws gently with cool water, keep them off hot ground, and **go to the vet**. Pad burns are easy to underestimate and they're painful — a vet visit is always the right call for anything beyond mild redness.

## A note for cat households

Indoor cats meet the same danger in smaller ways — the sunny windowsill, the balcony tile, the car bonnet they hop onto. The same rule of thumb protects them: if a surface is too hot for the back of your hand, keep paws off it.

The lovely thing about this one is how simple it is. Seven seconds of your time before each walk, all summer — and burnt paws simply never happen to your dog.

*Try the test on your own street today — you might be surprised how late in the evening the pavement stays hot.*
