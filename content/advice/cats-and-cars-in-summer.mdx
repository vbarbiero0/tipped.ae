When the streets hit 45°C, a parked car is one of the few pieces of shade a street cat can find. Cats rest under cars, on top of tyres, and — more often than you'd think — climb up into the engine bay itself, where it's dark and sheltered. It's a sensible choice from the cat's point of view. It's also the most dangerous spot in the neighbourhood the moment someone turns the key.

A ten-second habit protects them.

## Check before you drive

**Knock on the bonnet, twice, firmly.** The sound and vibration wake a sleeping cat and give them a moment to leave. Then pause before starting the engine — a cat needs a few seconds to climb down.

**Glance underneath.** A cat flat against the ground in the shade of your car is easy to miss from the driver's seat. A quick look under the front and back takes no time at all.

**Listen after you start.** If you hear anything unexpected — a cry, a thump, scratching — turn the engine off straight away and look. Don't drive "just a little" to check; open the bonnet.

If a cat is inside the engine bay and won't come out, patience works better than reaching in. Step back, keep things quiet, and give them a clear path to leave. A frightened cat exits on their own far faster than one being pulled.

## In the hot months, make it every time

From May to October, make the bonnet-knock as automatic as your seatbelt. Mornings matter most: cats settle into engine bays overnight while the metal is still warm, and they're often still asleep there when people leave for work. If you park in the same place daily and cats live nearby, assume you have company until proven otherwise.

## The puddle under the car isn't water for them

On humid days, air conditioners drip condensation, and a puddle forms under parked cars — often the only "water" on a dry street. Thirsty cats drink from it. The trouble is what else ends up in that puddle: engine residue, coolant traces and road grime, and coolant in particular is dangerously toxic to cats even in small amounts, while tasting sweet enough to invite them.

The fix is wonderfully simple: **give them something better.** A deep ceramic bowl of clean water in reliable shade, refreshed each morning, means the cats on your street never need the puddle. If you can, wipe up or wash away standing drips where cats gather.

## Know the danger signs

If you ever suspect a cat has been in contact with a moving engine or has drunk something under a car, don't wait to be sure:

- Wobbliness, or walking as if drunk
- Drooling or pawing at the mouth
- Visible wounds, limping, or a cat suddenly hiding and unreachable

Any of these means **a vet, now.** With suspected poisoning especially, the earlier a vet sees them, the more they can do.

None of this asks much — a knock, a glance, a bowl of clean water. For the cats who share your street, those small habits are the difference between a safe summer and a tragedy nobody wanted.

*Tonight, put a bowl of water somewhere shaded near where you park — you'll likely meet your neighbours within the week.*
