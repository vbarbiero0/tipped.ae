By June, the cats you feed are living in a very different city than the one you walk through. Pavement holds heat long after sunset, water bowls dry out by mid-morning, and the shaded corner that was perfect in March may be in full sun by noon. A few small changes to your routine can make the difference for the cats who depend on you.

## Water that lasts the day

**Choose ceramic or clay bowls, never metal.** Metal bowls heat up quickly and warm the water inside them; ceramic stays cooler and doesn't burn a nose or tongue at midday. Clay pots actually cool their contents a little as they breathe.

**Go deep, not wide.** A deep bowl holds its temperature and evaporates far more slowly than a shallow dish. Two or three deep bowls in different spots are better than one large tray — if one is tipped over or taken, the cats still have options.

**Refresh in the morning, before the heat.** Water put out at 6 or 7 am starts the day cool and clean. Topping up a hot bowl in the afternoon helps, but the morning refresh is the one that matters most.

## Shade that stays shade

Walk your feeding spots at different times of day if you can. A spot that's shaded at 8 am may be in direct sun by 1 pm, exactly when the cats need it most. Look for shade that lasts all day — the north side of buildings, covered parking, dense shrubs — and move bowls and food there.

## Feed at dawn and dusk

In summer, shift feeding to the coolest hours: early morning and after sunset. The cats are more comfortable moving then, and so are you.

**Wet food spoils fast in the heat** — at 45°C it can turn within an hour, and spoiled food causes stomach upsets the cats can't afford. In summer, either stay while they eat and take away what's left, or lean on dry food, which keeps safely through the day.

## Shelter for summer is about airflow

Winter shelters are cosy and enclosed. Summer shelter is the opposite: what a cat needs in the heat is **shade with air moving through it**. A raised platform in deep shade, an open-sided box up off the hot ground, a gap behind a planter where the breeze passes — these help. Anything insulated or enclosed traps heat and can be more dangerous than no shelter at all.

## Know the danger signs

Heat stress in cats is easy to miss because cats hide when they feel unwell. Watch for:

- **Panting or open-mouth breathing** — cats almost never pant; a panting cat needs help now
- **Drooling, or a wet chin**
- **Lethargy beyond normal summer laziness** — not responding to food, not moving away when approached
- **Stumbling or wobbliness**

If you see these, move the cat to shade, offer cool (not iced) water, wet their paws and ears gently — and **get them to a vet right away**. Heatstroke moves quickly, and the vet is always the right next step.

Most of all: keep going. The water you put out this morning, the shade you found last week — for the cats on your block, that quiet routine is the whole difference between a hard season and a safe one.
