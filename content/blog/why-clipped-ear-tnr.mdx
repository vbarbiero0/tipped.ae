Look closely at the street cats in your neighbourhood and you'll notice some of them share a small mark: the very tip of one ear, usually the left, is flat where it would naturally come to a point. It isn't an injury and it isn't a fight scar. It's one of the kindest marks in animal welfare — and it's where our name comes from.

## What the tipped ear means

While a cat is under anaesthesia for sterilisation, the vet removes the tip of one ear. It's painless, it heals quickly, and it lasts a lifetime. From then on, anyone — a feeder, a vet, a municipality worker, a neighbour — can see at a glance:

**This cat has been sterilised, vaccinated, and someone is looking after them.**

That visibility matters. It means the cat won't be trapped a second time for a surgery they've already had. It tells the neighbourhood this is a managed, healthy cat rather than a stray nobody knows. In a very real sense, the tipped ear is a cat's ID card.

## How trap-neuter-return works

TNR is exactly what the name says, done with care at each step:

**Trap.** A rescuer sets a safe, humane trap — usually baited with food, usually at dawn or dusk when the cats come to eat. The cats are frightened for a short while, never harmed.

**Neuter.** A vet sterilises the cat, vaccinates them, treats anything obvious, and tips the ear while the cat is asleep. Most cats stay one night to recover.

**Return.** The cat goes back to the exact spot they came from — their home territory, where they know the food, the shade and the safe places, and where their feeder is waiting.

## Why returning them works

It can seem strange at first: why go to all that trouble and then put the cat back on the street? The answer is that it's the only approach that actually works.

Street cats live in territories. Remove the cats from a block and new, unsterilised cats simply move into the empty space — and the cycle restarts. But a sterilised, vaccinated colony holds its territory while producing no kittens, so the numbers fall gently, generation by generation, without a single cat coming to harm. The cats that remain are healthier, fight less, and roam less. It's kinder, and it's more effective — the rare pair of qualities that go together.

Kittens and friendly adults, meanwhile, don't go back at all: they go into foster care and up for adoption. That's where [the pets you'll meet on tipped](/pets) come from — the ones ready for a home, saved by the same rescuers who run the TNR rounds.

## Why we're named after it

Every tipped ear is an animal someone saved: trapped gently, treated properly, returned safely, fed daily. It's quiet work, done at dawn by ordinary people at their own expense, and most of the city never notices it happening.

We named the platform after that mark because it's the whole story in one small sign — proof that somebody cared enough to act.

## How you can help

You don't need to trap cats to be part of this. Feed a colony regularly. Put out water in the summer. [Support a rescuer's supply wishlist](/rescuers), or help pay a clinic bill directly — every dirham goes to the vet, never through us. And if a cat with a *pointed* ear keeps visiting your street, mention it to a local rescuer; they'll know what to do.

*Next time you spot a tipped ear on your street, you'll know: that small flat edge is somebody's promise, kept.*
